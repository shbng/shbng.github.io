Rotations. As with any geometric generalisation, we start from a low-dimensional Euclidean intuition. These are the rigid transformations of an object. One could ask two questions,


- *Which rotations preserve this object?*
- *What rotations are allowed for this object?*


The first corresponds to an embedding. It relies on the existence of an ambient space which has *all* available rotations, and ask which of these are the symmetries of the embedded object. The second is however an intrinsic question, purely relying on the transformations defined only on the object under consideration. As with much of geometry, the intrinsic study becomes important to avoid dealing with any extraneous information at times and isolate the geometric properties which depend solely on the metric. 

Following an intrinsic definition, I would like to ask the following questions.

1. *How to stitch together the isotropic information at every point to get the isometry group of the manifold? Since, isotropies only capture rotational symmetries, we might need some more translational information.*

2. *This motivates us to ask firstly, what are the conditions on a manifold to make sure every isometry has a fixed point, i.e. all isomtries are precisely the isotropies at some point? Compactness is a must...*
 
3. *Assuming we got rid of translations, how can we stitch together isotropies to get the global isomteries.*

4. *Using the information of the isometry group, what can we say about the geometry/topology of the manifolds?*
