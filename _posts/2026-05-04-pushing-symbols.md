Yes, math is mostly about pushing symbols and reaching answers, questions and all of that. But it is mostly natural too! There is so much going on behind those symbol pushing. We can almost *feel* it, the process of math becomes then to rigorously put everything down. So yes, symbol pushing, sometimes unpredictably perhaps and sometimes predictably, but there seems to be a context almost always. And that's why it's a science. Because there is *meaning* behind those results and proofs - behind those symbol pushings.

Sometimes, we need to stay grounded and move with symbol pushing. I mean being content with *only pushing sybmols*. That's the analogous struggles to *vagueness in physics*. But in any subject, there are layers of understanding things, and one can never understand them fully at once. So understanding requires a lot of context. Thankfully, math is organised and rigorous, so it's possible to reach that context down the line wihtout a fuzz. It can be hard, and can be part of research too I guess, but it is rigorously possible. I won't comment about the vagueness in physics. It's an art to deal with it. And to deal with it without crumbling any scientific spirits is just unimaginable for me. So yeah, respect to physicists. 

But yeah, I will stick to the beauty and the terrors of math :). It's too good. 


And, in any given sitting/chalking/thought-walking, it takes time to internalise the results/proofs/concepts to feel the naturaliy of everything. Not going through that process becomes again just symbol pushing. May be geniuses can workout everything without internalising things, or perhaps internalise without much effort. But I think on a general level, the process of going through a bachelor's/master's training is that internalisation of fundamental ideas in math and physics. It takes time. And it must be respected. Atleast this whole thing aids in preserving humanness in the scholarly world. Which can involve teaching, communication, engagement and research.

This takes me back to the time when Ashok Sen talked about internalisation too! That day, in the SPS Discuss hall at NISER.He emphasised the difference between the process of *memorising* and *internalising*. That really resonated with me! 

So yeah, it's important to sit with things. I just finished the section on dual spaces and the interaction between linear maps and their dual ones. The results are pretty much symbol pushing at first glance. It's not very conceptual, neither immediately natural at first glance. At least, I didn't read them with an energy to pause and slow down at every instant. But sometimes, it's also good to cruise through such non-conceptual things (squeeze in a little win ;)) and then sit with the things to internalise them. So, I am gonna do that now before I move on to solve problems on this. 

Of course, time has also taught me that, a lot of the internalisation happens through problems as well. So, there are layers of this, and the goal is move between those, trying to do justice to whateveer we are doing in the present. So, right now there is some slowing down to do with the concepts, and then the next process of problem-solving shall begun.

I think this process of internalisation can be very personal to an individual too. There are some rational layers one can keep in mind, but the weights and dynamics can probably fuzz around. For example, I realised I tend to spend time on concepts a little more than usual, before I jump into problems. Although, doing it too much ignoring the potential the problems and simply moving ahead sometimes hold in internalising things, is naive; I do claim however that there's a healthy level of spending time with concepts alone. Again, atleast for me :). It opens my brain-lid to allow some of my own questions around the concepts to appear. It's rewarding (both personally and mathematically) to sit with those. And it's absolutely thrilling to witness those connections and internalisations dance with the next phase of problem-solving. Especially when some of those very processings/questions appear directly as exercise problems later on! This whole dance is just amazing in my experience.
