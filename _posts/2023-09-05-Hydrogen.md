## Baby Hydrogen atom
Let's first recall some basic quantum mechanical ideas and methods that govern any such scenario.

For a spherically symmetric system like Hydrogen -- with a single electron under the influence of proton field from the center viz. $V \sim V(r)$ -- the schrodinger equation takes the special form, splitting the wavefunction into Radial and angular parts:

$$ \psi\left( r,\theta,\phi \right) = R(r)Y\left( \theta, \phi \right). $$

Where, we already separated the time and spatial varaibles since the potential is independent of time. We solve for $\psi$ using the *time-independent Schrodinger equation*:

$$ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla ^2\psi + V\psi = E\psi,$$

$\psi$ being the spatial wavefunction and $E$ the energy. The radial part $u(r) \equiv rR(r)$ then obeys,

$$ - \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2u}{dr^2} + \left[ V + \frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{l\left( l+1 \right)}{r^2}\right]u = Eu.$$ 

The angular part obeys the laplace equation in spherical domain, and is solved independent of the potential $V(r)$ by the spherical harmonics,

$$ Y_l^m\left(\theta,\phi\right) = \sqrt{\frac{\left( 2l+1 \right) }{4\pi} \frac{\left( l-m \right)! }{\left( l+m \right)!}}e^{im\phi}P_l^m\left( \cos\theta \right).$$ 

$P_l^m$ is the associated Legendre function, $l = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ appears as a separation constant, and $m = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots$ appears due to the $\phi \to  \phi + 2\pi$ symmetry.

With the hydrogen atom potential, $V = -\frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{1}{r}$, the radial part solves to determine the allowed energy levels:

$$E_n = - \left[\frac{m_e}{2\hbar^2}\left(\frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\right)^2\right]\cdot \frac{1}{n^2}, ~~~ n = 1,2,3,\ldots$$

with a ground state of $-13.6 eV.$

We find that for an arbitrary $n$ above, the possible values of $l$ include $0,1,\ldots,n-1$ and for each $l$ we have $(2l + 1)$ possible values for $m: -l,\ldots,0,\ldots,l$. This gives a degeneracy in each energy level $n$ to be,

$$deg.(n) = \sum_{l=0}^{n-1} (2l + 1) = n^2.$$

We can summarise the entirety of this Hydrogen atom via the following form of the spatial wavefunction, and the different quatum numbers.

$$\begin{align*}
\psi_{nlm}(r,\theta,\phi) = A \left(\frac{r}{a_0}\right)^l \left[1 +  \beta\left(\frac{r}{a_0}\right) + \ldots + (\sim)\left(\frac{r}{a_0}\right)^N\right] \\
\times \exp\left({-\frac{r}{na_0}}\right)\cdot Y_{lm}(\theta,\phi),
\end{align*}$$

$$n = N + l + 1, \quad N = 0,1,2,\ldots,$$

$$l = 0,1,\ldots,n-1, \quad m = -l,\ldots,-,\ldots,l.$$

$a_0$ is the Bohr radius, which is defined to be $\frac{\hbar^2}{me^2} \sim 53pm$ is an important length scale. It is basically the most probably distance between the nucleus and the electron in the ground state of Hydrogen atom.

There is a more transparent way to write these energy levels in terms of the fine structure constant $\alpha$, 

$$E_n = -\frac{e^2}{2a_0}\left(\frac{1}{n^2}\right) = -\frac{1}{2}\alpha^2mc^2\left(\frac{1}{n^2}\right),\quad \alpha_{\text{fine structure}} = \frac{e^2}{\hbar c} \simeq \frac{1}{137}.$$

We can also read off the momentum scale (upto an Heisenberg's uncertainity), 

$$p \sim \frac{\hbar}{a_0} = \alpha (mc) = m(\alpha c)$$

which gives a characterstic speed scale for the electron to be around $\frac{c}{137}$ and also justifies ever so slightly, our non-relativistic treament ($\gamma \approx 1.0000266 $) of this atom.

This gives us a very good base -- on top of which we can try adding other interactions, like magentic fields and also introduce a relativistic coorections. We did the easiest estimate of dynamics quantum mechanically. Of course, the early non-quantum estimates were remarkable too! Especially the stimates due to Bohr.


## The algebra of Hydrogen atom

The algebraic methods in quantum mechanics are far reaching and it's important to recap some of it in the context of Hydrogen atom. Not only that the algebraic structure is powerful on its own, it often also gives us a way to formalise and gain insights on the more abstruse elements of solving a Schrodinger equation. In particular, the symmetries can be captured more accessibly, afterall that's how the study of *abstract algebra* was born.  

Here, by algebra, we mostly mean the algebra of angular momentum that's going to play a crucial role in reconciling the fine structure. 

### Angular Momentum algebra -------

Energy and angular momentum are the fundamental conserved quantities of a classical system. In our solutions to the quantum equations, the quantum numbers $l,m$ are related to the angular momentum. The angular momentum operators read,

$$L_x = yp_z - zp_y,~L_y = zp_x - xp_z,~L_z = xp_y - yp_x$$

where we promote $p_i \to -i\hbar \partial/\partial x_i$. Commutation relations between these operators help us build the strucutre of spherical symmetry through raising and lowering operators, wihtout any differential analysis. The summary is that,
$[L_i,L_j] = i\hbar L_k$

$L_i, L_j$ are not simultaneously diagonalizable which could give us their eigensstates, but instead $L^2$ and $L_i$ commute. A structure proliferates from these operators as follows.

$$L^2f^m_l = \hbar^2\ell(\ell+1)f^m_l; \quad L_zf^m_l = \hbar mf^m_l$$

where

$$l = 0, 1/2, 1, 3/2, \ldots; \quad m = -\ell, \ell+1, \ldots, \ell.$$

Infact, writing the operator $L$ in polar coordinates, we get back precsiely the spherical laplace equation with solutions $Y^m_l$. This tells us $f^m_l = Y^m_l$, but more crucially algebraic construction gives an expanded range of values for $l$ (half-integers) not captured by the differential situation. Infact, this algebraic construction helps us to model the *intrinsic spin* that every elementary particle carries in a fixed manner. The spin operators $S_x,S_y,S_z$ are defined to have the same commutators as of $L$ but without any differential definition. The maximum $S_z$, call $s$ turns out to be fixed for any particle, and for electron, $s = 1/2$. 

This is yet another kind of additional interaction one can add in our previous solutions. However, for a start we only use the 2-fold degeneracy ($1/2 - 1, 1/2$) for electrons without any interaction with the proton field to give a degeneracy twice as much as we obtained before. This makes the degeneracy of each $n^{th}$ level $2n^2$, which are actually indicated brackets in the figure of $H$-spectrum.

For spin-1/2, the structure of spin operators is standardized by taking the eigenstates to be spin-up and spin-down states, represented 
$\begin{pmatrix}
1  \\ 
 0 \end{pmatrix}$
 and in this basis we write $S = \hbar/2 \sigma$, where

$$\sigma_x = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \sigma_y = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \sigma_z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}.$$

These are called the Pauli matrices, and form a 2-dimensional representation of the spin algebra. 

### Spin, orbital, total angular momentum - basis. Zwe -------
  
We could express our states using $(n,l,m,m_s)$, where $m_z$ is the z-component ($\pm 1/2$) of the intrinsic spin ($s=1/2$). This is usually refered to as the *uncoupled basis*, where as the *coupled basis* is obtained by adding the spin $(s,m_s)$ and orbital angular momentum $(l,m_l)$ to give the total angular momentum $(j,m_j)$. The addition of angular momenta for the hydrogen atom reads,

$$\ell \otimes \frac{1}{2} = L_{|\ell - 1/2|} \oplus L_{|\ell + 1/2|}$$

i.e. the resulting momenta are either of $j = \| \ell - 1/2  \|$ or $j = (\ell + 1/2)$ with their corresponding $m_j$ 's. We represent hydrogen atom states in this summed basis (coupled basis) as $(n,\ell,j,m_j)$ or by supressing the $m_j$ degenerac, simply by $nL_j$. $L$ represents the orbital $\ell$. ## Baby Dirac equation

For a loop carrying a current I, we write the magnetic moment $\bar{\mu} = \frac{I \bar{A}}{C}$. So, an electron with orbital angular momentum $\bar{L}$ has $\bar{\mu} = \frac{q}{2mC}\bar{L}$.

Being reasonably intuitive, people have tried writing $\bar{\mu} = g \frac{e}{2m_eC} \bar{S}$ for the magnetic moment due to the quantum spin of electron. Clearly we were ambitious about the nature of magnetic moment in quantum regime in writing it almost same as classical one. But we have also parametrized all our ignorance in the factor $g$. Experiments show that $g \approx 2$, a simple *2* for the electron! So quantum spin is not that strange from classical after all! But nevertheless this is the first glance at why the quantum spin is not about electron spinning around it's axis, although telling us its probably okay to think on those lines (with a whispering $g$ accompanying every time).  

So, Pauli in 1927 went on to formulate this whole business. He noticed something funny, 

  \begin{align}
  (\bar{\sigma} .\bar{a}) (\bar{\sigma} .\bar{b}) = (\bar{a}.\bar{b} +i\bar{\sigma}.
  (\bar{a}\times\bar{b}))
  \end{align}

  <p>which tells us $(\bar{\sigma} .\bar{p})^2 = \bar{p}^2$. With prior learning from Stern-Gerlach eperiment regarding the two spin states of electron, he started out with
  writing the Hamiltonian eigenstate as a column vector, a 2-spinor $\psi = \begin{bmatrix} \chi \\ \eta \end{bmatrix}$.</p>


Rewriting the Hamiltonian this way replacing $\bar{p}^2$ with $(\bar{\sigma}.\bar{p})^2$ and in present of the E.M fields, $\bar{p} \rightarrow \bar{\pi} = \bar{p} - \frac{q}{C}\bar{A}$, we have 

 <p>
 $$\begin{align}
H_{Pauli} &= \frac{1}{2m} \left[ \bar{\sigma} \cdot \left(\bar{p} - \frac{q}{c}\bar{A}\right) \right]^2 + q\phi  \\
&= \frac{1}{2m} \left(\bar{p} - \frac{q}{c}\bar{A}\right)^2 - \frac{q\hbar}{2mc} \bar{\sigma} \cdot \bar{B} + q\phi  \\
&= \frac{1}{2m} \left[ \bar{p}^2 - \frac{q}{c}(\bar{L} + 2\bar{S}) \cdot \bar{B} \right] + q\phi \\
&= \frac{(\bar{\sigma}\cdot\bar{\pi})(\bar{\sigma}\cdot\bar{\pi})}{2m}
\end{align}$$ 
</p>

Where we have assumed magnetic field is weak in writing the third line. The g-factor is naturally
seen to be 2 in the Pauli equation as the coefficient of $\bar{S}$. We will see that this takes a familiar form in the presence of electromagnetic fields.


## The Dirac equation
The dynamics of Electromagnetic interaction is thus layed out well due to Pauli, while also explaining the Lande's g-factor for the electron! Then, in 1928, Dirac figured a way to blend the relativistic aspects correctly with the Pauli's equation. There have been many historic issues in relativistic formulation that physicists faced which ultimately lead to QFT. I divert all such subtleties to articles in the <i><a href="">Ideas of Quantum Field Theory</a></i> series. I will thus assume that *the Dirac equation* is a nice way to reconcile the relativistic mechanics in the quantum world. Essentially, to escape the infinite orders of terms when one expands the relativistic energy, Dirac wrote $p^2C^2 + m^2C^4$ as a square of some linear function of $p$. Clearly there are no mixing terms, so Dirac went crazy. He wrote,

$$p^2C^2 + m^2C^4 = (C\bar{\alpha}.\bar{p} + \beta mC^2)$$

Doing the obvious sanity checks leaves us with 

$$(\alpha_i)^2 = \beta^2 = \mathbb{1};\quad \alpha_i\beta + \beta\alpha_i = 0$$

in terms of the four objects $\alpha_i$ and $\beta$. 

$$\alpha_i\alpha_j + \alpha_j\alpha_i = 0$$

 With a dimensional discussion on when such properties can be satisfied, one ends up needing to start with with a 4 dimensional representation, constructed out of Pauli matrices $\sigma$. 

<p>
\begin{align}
\bar{\alpha} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & \bar{\sigma} \\
\bar{\sigma} & 0
\end{bmatrix}; \quad\beta = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbb{1} & 0 \\ 0 & -\mathbb{1}\end{bmatrix}
\end{align}
</p>

Basically we can't have a two dimensional representation because, the pauli matrices are the natural choice which needs the identity matrix to form a basis. Which then breaks the required algebra. A trace argument eliminates $3\times3$ (any $odd \times odd$ dimensional) matrices altogether. 


We usually combine these four matrices and denote them $\set{\gamma_\mu},~ \mu = 0,1,2,3$, with:

$$ \alpha_i = \gamma^0\gamma^i, \quad \beta = \gamma^0.$$

We have what's called the *Clifford Algebra* in terms of these $\gamma$'s,

$$ \frac{1}{2}\{\gamma^\mu,\gamma^\nu\} = \eta^{\mu\nu}.$$

Now, the Hamiltonian reads,

$$H = \bar{\alpha}\cdot\bar{p}c + \beta mc^2.$$ 

and the Dirac equation,

$$(i\gamma^\mu\partial_\mu - m)\Psi(\bar{x},t) = 0.$$

This is our starting point for a relativistic treatment of hydrogen atom (still not a local/causal theory! essentially not a field theory yet).

## Fine Structure of Hydrogen

We aim to solve the Dirac equation $H\psi = E\psi$ interms of two 2-component spinors in the overall Dirac 4-spinor $\psi$ on the which we have the 4-dimensional $\gamma$-matrices acting naturally,

<p>$$\psi = \begin{bmatrix}\chi \\ \eta\end{bmatrix}, \quad \chi = \begin{bmatrix}\chi_1 \\ \chi_2\end{bmatrix}, \eta = \begin{bmatrix}\eta_1 \\ \eta_2\end{bmatrix}.$$</p>

After some linear algebraic manipulations, we see that $\eta$ is very small compared to $\chi$ and,

$$E'\chi := (E - mc^2)\chi = \frac{(\sigma\cdot p)(\sigma \cdot p)}{2m}\chi.$$

In an electromagnetic background, we replace $E' \to E' - q\phi$ and $\bar{p} \to \bar{\pi} = \bar{p} - \frac{q}{c}\bar{A}$, and get the new energy equation

$$(E' - q\phi)\chi = \frac{(\sigma\cdot \bar{\pi})K(\sigma \cdot \bar{\pi})}{2m}\chi$$

where $K = \frac{2mc^2}{E' + 2mc^2 - q\phi}$.

The above equation upto an order of $(v/c)^2$, gives the Pauli Hamiltonian. To read out a hamiltonian upto order $(v/c)^4$, we must decouple the two-component spinors of the 4-component Dirac spinor $\psi$. This is done by what's called *Foldy–Wouthuysen transformation* $\Omega$ which is a unitary transformation $\Omega$ given by,

$$\Omega = e^{iS}, \quad S = \beta\frac{\alpha\cdot\pi}{2mc}.$$

The finer details of this calculation can be found in for example here - [UC Berkeley, Phy 221B Notes](https://bohr.physics.berkeley.edu/classes/221/notes/foldyw.pdf). 

We use

$$\chi = \Omega^{-1}\psi, \quad \Omega^{-1} \approx 1 - \frac{(\sigma\cdot\pi)^2}{8m^2c^2}.$$

to simplify the Dirac equation to $(v/c)^4$ order, and the new Hamiltonian reads,

<p>
\begin{align*}
H = & \underbrace{\frac{\hat{p}^2}{2m} + V}_{\text{Schrödinger}} - \underbrace{\frac{\hat{p}^4}{8m^3c^2}}_{\delta H_{\text{rel}}} \\ & + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2m^2c^2}\frac{1}{r}\frac{dV}{dr}(\vec{S}\cdot\vec{L})}_{\delta H_{\text{SO}}} + \underbrace{\frac{\hbar^2}{8m^2c^2}\nabla^2V}_{\delta H_{\text{Darwin}}}
\end{align*}
</p>

This is the Hamiltonian that explains the *fine structure* of Hydrogen spectrum, that was observed experimentally and ridlled physicists for a long time. We refer to this Hamiltonian as the *fine structure Hamitlonian* from now on. This includes the baby hydrogen atom, the relativistic correction, the spin and orbital coupling (intrinsic spin interacting with the orbital electromagnetism), and finally the Darwin term. The Darwin term is related to the fact that electron is not just a point particle but a fuzz of charge that is distributed locally! 

## Perturbation Theory

A good application of time-independent perturbation theory is now due here. One of the important takeaways of non-degenerate perturbation theory is that, the first order correction in energy due to a perturbation $\delta H$ is given by,

$$E^{(1)} = \langle \psi^{(0)} \mid \delta H \mid \psi^{(0)}\rangle.$$

And one of the convenient subtleties of the *degenerate* perturbation theory is that, once we find a *good* basis of states, we can use non-degenerate perturbation theory on these states (of equal energies) to compute the energy corrections. For example, we will soon see that the coupled basis for Hydrogen atom forms a *good basis* for the spin-orbital pertubration $\delta H_{SO}$. And it is in this basis that the fine structure energy correction is usually expressed. By the way, the spin orbit coupling is also referred to as $LS$ coupling for obvious reasons. Let's now look at each of the perturbations and their energy corrections.


### Darwin Term
We tackle the easiest one first. Specialised to the $V(r) = -e^2/r$ for Hydrogen atom, the Darwin term reads,

$$\delta H = \frac{\pi}{2}\frac{e^2\hbar^2}{m^2c^2}\delta(\bar{r}).$$

That is, it's only contribution to the correction is at $r=0$. Since our Hydrogen atom has non-trivial wave function at $r=0$ only for $l=0$ orbitals, the energy correction reads,

<p>
\begin{align*}
E^{(1)}_{\text{Darwin}} &= \langle \psi_{n00}\mid\delta H \mid \psi_{n00}\rangle \\
&= \frac{\pi e^2 \hbar^2}{2m^2c^2} |\psi_{n00}|^2 \\
&= \alpha^4 mc^2 \left(\frac{1}{2n^3}\right). 
\end{align*}
</p>

One can derive this correction independently of the Dirac's by accounting the slightly varied potential experienced by the electron, due to its *distribution* of charge. We may replace our old potential $V(r)$ with 

$$\widetilde{V}(\bar{r}) = \int_{e^-}d^3u \rho_0(\bar{u})V(\bar{r} + \bar{u})$$

accomodating a non-trivial charge density $\rho_0$ spread over a compton radius $\sim \hbar/mc$. This model is ofcourse not precise, but the resulting estimate is very close (within a factor of 4/5) to the above Darwin term!

$$\widetilde{V}(\bar{r}) = V(r) + \frac{1}{10}\frac{\hbar^2}{m^2c^2}\nabla^2V.$$

This makes the origin of Darwin term more transparent. Dirac's equation cacounts for this charge distribution intrinsically!

### Relativistic Correction

In this case, our usual $(n,\ell,m_\ell)$ basis is indeed good since

$$\left[\bar{p}^4,\bar{L}^2\right] = 0, \quad \left[\bar{p}^4,\bar{J}_z\right].$$

So we safely adopt the non-degenrate theory as follows,

<p>
\begin{align*}
E^{(1)\text{rel}}_{n \ell m} = -\frac{1}{8m^3c^2}\left\langle \psi_{n\ell m_\ell} \mid \hat{p}^4  \mid \psi_{n\ell m_\ell} \right\rangle
\end{align*}
</p>

Here, the situation is even simpler. The state $\Psi_{nlm}$ are precisely the eigenstates of $L^2$ and $L_z$, thus eigenstates of $\delta H_{rel}$ too. So, we must have same $\ell, m_\ell$ values for the correction to be non-zero! Thus,

<p>
\begin{align*}
E^{(1)rel}_{n \ell m_\ell} &= -\frac{1}{8m^3c^2} \langle p^2 \psi_{n\ell m} \mid p^2 \psi_{n\ell m} \rangle \\
&= -\frac{1}{2m c^2} \langle (E_n - V(r)) \psi_{n\ell m} \mid (E_n - V(r)) \psi_{n\ell m} \rangle \\
 &= -\frac{1}{8} \alpha^4 (mc^2) \left\{ \frac{4n}{\left(\ell + \frac{1}{2}\right)} - 3 \right\} 
\end{align*}
</p>

### Spin-Orbit Coupling

In this case, $\delta H \propto \bar{L}\cdot \bar{S} = \frac{1}{2}\left(J^2 - L^2 - S^2\right)$ which actually commutes with $L^2, J^2$ and $J_z$! Hence, as we mentioned earlier, $(n,l,j,m_j)$ is a good basis and the energy correction reads,

<p>
\begin{align*}
E_{n\,l\,j\,m_j}^{(1)\,\text{SO}} &= \frac{e^2}{2m^2c^2} \left\langle n\,l\,j\,m_j \left| \frac{\vec{S}\cdot\vec{L}}{r^3} \right| n\,l\,j\,m_j \right\rangle \\
&= \frac{\left(E_n^{(0)}\right)^2}{mc^2} n \cdot \left\{ \frac{j(j+1) - l(l+1) - \frac{3}{4}}{l\left(l+\frac{1}{2}\right)(l+1)} \right\}
\end{align*}
</p>

Note that no correction among three depends on $m_\ell$ or $m_j$! Which also allows us to combine the results of relativistic and spin-orbital corrections. 

The final energy levels of Hydrogen atom after including this fine structure are thus,

$$E_{nj} = -\frac{13.6eV}{n^2} \left(1+ \frac{\alpha^2}{n^2}\left(\frac{n}{j+1/2} - \frac{3}{4}\right)\right)$$

without the Darwin term, which also reads,

$$E_n^{(1)}\vert_{\text{Darwin}} = \alpha^4 mc^2 \left(\frac{1}{2n^3}\right).$$

All the energy corrections are of the order $\alpha^4$, with the unperturbed energy being $\propto \alpha^2.$ The constant $\alpha$ (called the fine structure constant) indeed appears in fundamental calculations in QFT, where the renormalized results (loop diagrams) like electron self-energy, Lande's g-factor are expressed in orders of $\alpha$. Over final destination with this article is to calculate what's known as *Lamb shift* in the energy levels of Hydrogen atom. This appears because of electron self-interaction and vacuum interactions calculated using the above mentioned ideas of QFT.
